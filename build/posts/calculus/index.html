Introduction

The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus connects differentiation and integration, showing that these two operations are essentially inverse processes.

The First Fundamental Theorem

If f is continuous on [a,b] and F is defined by:

F(x) = \int_a^x f(t) \, dt

then F is differentiable on (a,b) and:

F'(x) = f(x)

The Second Fundamental Theorem

If f is continuous on [a,b] and F is any antiderivative of f, then:

\int_a^b f(x) \, dx = F(b) - F(a)

Examples

Example 1

Find the derivative of: g(x) = \int_0^x \sin(t^2) \, dt

By the First Fundamental Theorem: g'(x) = \sin(x^2)

Example 2

Evaluate: \int_1^4 (2x + 3) \, dx

An antiderivative is F(x) = x^2 + 3x, so: \int_1^4 (2x + 3) \, dx = F(4) - F(1) = (16 + 12) - (1 + 3) = 24

Applications

The Fundamental Theorem has many applications:

  • Computing definite integrals

  • Solving differential equations

  • Physics problems involving rates of change

  • Economics and optimization

Proof Sketch

The proof relies on the Mean Value Theorem and the definition of the derivative. The key insight is that:

\frac{F(x+h) - F(x)}{h} = \frac{1}{h} \int_x^{x+h} f(t) \, dt

As h \to 0, this approaches f(x) by continuity.

Conclusion

The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus is one of the most important results in mathematics, providing the foundation for much of modern analysis and its applications.
